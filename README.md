Figure 1 – Homepage Wireframe
The homepage uses a structured top-to-bottom layout beginning with a fixed navigation bar, followed by a large hero banner, featured products grid, farmer profiles, a benefits section, and a newsletter signup above the footer. The hero banner dominates the upper portion of the page, immediately communicating the platform's purpose with a bold headline and a clear "Shop Local Produce" call-to-action button. Important elements such as the CTA button, product cards, and section headings are visually prioritised through size, colour contrast, and spacing, guiding the user's eye down the page naturally. The navigation bar follows standard e-commerce conventions, including links to Browse Products, Producers, Loyalty Rewards, and Account, making it immediately familiar to users of platforms like Amazon Fresh or Ocado. This page allows customers to discover the platform, understand its value proposition, and quickly navigate to product browsing or producer information.

Figure 2 – Product Browsing Page Wireframe
The browsing page is divided into a two-column layout, with a left-hand sidebar containing filters and a main content area displaying a grid of product cards. The search bar sits prominently at the top of the content area, ensuring it is the first interactive element a user encounters when looking for specific items. Product cards use consistent sizing and a clear hierarchy — product image at the top, followed by name, producer, price, and an "Add to Cart" button — so users can scan and compare items at a glance. The sidebar filter panel follows standard e-commerce conventions seen on sites like ASOS or eBay, allowing users to narrow results by category, price range, producer, and availability. This page allows customers to explore the full product catalogue, apply filters to find relevant items, and add products to their cart directly from the grid.

Figure 3 – Product Detail Page Wireframe
The product detail page uses a two-column layout at the top, with a large product image and thumbnail strip on the left, and all key purchase information — name, rating, price, stock level, badges, and the Add to Cart button — on the right. The product name and price are displayed in the largest text on the page, establishing an immediate visual hierarchy that prioritises the two most important pieces of information for a buying decision. Stock level indicators, organic badges, and star ratings are placed directly beneath the price, providing contextual trust signals before the user reaches the purchase action. A tabbed content area below the main section follows a widely recognised pattern used by retailers such as John Lewis and Waitrose, separating Description, Producer Info, Delivery, and Reviews without cluttering the page. This page allows customers to evaluate a product in full, read about the producer, and confidently complete a purchase.

Figure 4 – Customer Dashboard Wireframe
The customer dashboard uses a fixed left-hand sidebar for navigation alongside a main content area divided into clearly labelled sections: stat cards, order history, shipment tracking, loyalty points, and account settings. Key summary figures — total orders, items in transit, loyalty points, and average rating — are displayed as prominent stat cards at the top of the main content area, giving users an immediate snapshot of their account activity. The shipment tracking section uses a visual progress bar with labelled stages, making the delivery status instantly understandable without requiring users to read detailed text. The layout follows conventions used in dashboards on platforms like Deliveroo and ASOS, where a persistent sidebar provides consistent navigation and the main area updates contextually. This page allows customers to review their order history, track active deliveries, manage loyalty points, and update personal or delivery details.

Figure 5 – Producer Dashboard Wireframe
The producer dashboard mirrors the customer dashboard's sidebar-plus-main-content structure but is tailored to business management tasks, organising the main area into KPI metrics, a product management table, an add-product form, and an incoming orders panel. Revenue, order count, active listings, and average rating are surfaced immediately at the top as headline KPI cards, allowing producers to assess their performance at a glance without navigating elsewhere. The product table uses colour-coded status badges — green for live listings and amber for out-of-stock — to draw attention to items requiring action, supporting efficient stock management. The add-product form and orders table follow standard back-office UI conventions familiar from platforms like Shopify or Etsy's seller hub, reducing the learning curve for new producers. This page allows producers to manage their entire product catalogue, monitor incoming orders, and track sales performance from a single centralised view.

Figure 6 – Login / Sign Up Page Wireframe
The login page uses a split two-column layout: a branded left panel featuring the Greenfield identity, key platform benefits, and a customer testimonial, alongside a right panel containing the authentication forms. The right panel leads with a tabbed switcher between Sign In and Create Account, ensuring users can immediately identify which action they need without scrolling or navigating away. The Sign In form is compact and focused, with email, password, a forgot password link, and social login options presented in a logical vertical flow that minimises friction. The Create Account form introduces an account type toggle — Customer or Producer — following a convention used by marketplace platforms such as Etsy and eBay to distinguish between buyer and seller journeys from the point of registration. This page allows both new and returning users to access their accounts quickly, with the split layout reinforcing the platform's brand identity throughout the authentication process.




Here are brief explanations of each diagram:

Image 1 – Entity Relationship Diagram (ERD)
This diagram shows the database structure for Greenfield Local Hub. It maps out six tables — Users, Orders, Producers, Products, OrderItems, and LoyaltyRewards — and the relationships between them. For example, a User can be linked to a Producer profile, a Producer supplies Products, and Orders contain multiple OrderItems. The LoyaltyRewards table connects back to Users to track points balances and tier status.

Image 2 – Use Case Diagram
This diagram shows what each type of user can do on the platform. It is divided into three actor roles: Customer (register, log in, browse products, place orders, track shipments, earn rewards), Producer (view orders, add products, edit product details, update stock levels), and Admin (manage users, manage producers, manage the catalogue, resolve disputes, view platform analytics).

Image 3 – Class Diagram
This diagram shows the object-oriented structure of the system. It defines the key classes — Customer, User, Producer, Order, OrderItem, Product, and LoyaltyAccount — along with their attributes and methods. For instance, the Order class holds methods like addItem() and calculateTotal(), while the LoyaltyAccount class handles accruePoints() and redeemPoints(). Customer and Producer both generalise from the base User class.

Image 4 – Data Flow Diagram (DFD) Level 0
This diagram shows how data moves through the entire Greenfield Local Hub system. The central Marketplace Database acts as the core data store, feeding information to and from five main processes: Manage Account, Process Orders, Manage Products, Update Loyalty Rewards, and Track Shipments. Customers interact with account management, order processing, loyalty rewards, and shipment tracking, while Producers interact primarily with order notifications and product management. An optional Shipping Carrier external entity feeds delivery updates back into the tracking process.
